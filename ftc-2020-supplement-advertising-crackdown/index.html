The FTC's 2020 Crackdown on Supplement Claims Was Long Overdue - Here's What It Actually Changed

In 2020, the FTC ramped up enforcement on supplement advertising. Dr. James Holloway breaks down what happened, why it mattered, and what it didn't fix.

What the FTC Actually Did in 2020 - and What the Letters Said

Between April and October 2020, the FTC and FDA issued joint warning letters to more than 30 companies. These weren't fines or injunctions. They were formal notices requiring companies to remove specific claims or face further enforcement action. And the claims being targeted were, in many cases, genuinely egregious - products being positioned as treatments or preventives for COVID-19 specifically, which crossed from supplement territory into outright drug claim territory under FDA rules.

The FTC's authority here comes from Section 5 of the FTC Act, which prohibits unfair or deceptive acts in commerce. For supplements, that means advertising claims need to be substantiated by 'competent and reliable scientific evidence' - and the standard for what counts as competent is higher than a single rat study or a mechanistic hypothesis. In my work with athlete nutrition programs, I've watched brands routinely cite in vitro data (cells in a dish) as if it directly translates to human performance outcomes. It doesn't. And the 2020 enforcement wave, to its credit, applied that standard more visibly than it had in years prior.

The letters specifically called out claims like 'proven to kill COVID-19,' 'boosts immunity to fight coronavirus,' and similar language. Some companies complied within days. Others required follow-up action. The FTC flagged companies selling everything from colloidal silver to oleander-based supplements, and at least one case resulted in a temporary restraining order.

Why the Immune-Support Category Became the Flashpoint

Zinc, vitamin D, vitamin C, elderberry, quercetin - by March 2020, you couldn't scroll through a supplement retailer's website without seeing all of them positioned as COVID-adjacent solutions. Some of this was opportunistic nonsense. But some of it was genuinely complicated, because a few of these ingredients do have meaningful mechanistic roles in immune function. Zinc, for example, has reasonable evidence supporting its role in reducing duration of the common cold - a 2015 Cochrane Review found zinc lozenges shortened cold duration by about 33% in adults. That's a real, replicated finding. The problem is the leap from 'zinc supports normal immune function' to 'zinc protects against SARS-CoV-2,' which requires clinical evidence that simply didn't exist in 2020.

I'm not 100% sure this works for everyone, but I genuinely respect the brands that held the line and kept their claims accurate during that period - there weren't many of them, and I remember one brand director I spoke to in May 2020 who told me his marketing team was furious with him for refusing to add 'immune defense' language to their zinc product. He kept saying 'we can't claim that, we don't have the data,' and honestly, that conversation stuck with me because it illustrated exactly how much internal pressure exists to stretch the truth. My friend who works in supplement retail told me around the same time that customers were asking for 'the COVID vitamin' by name and pointing at vitamin D displays. The demand was real. The temptation to meet it with inflated claims was obviously enormous.

The FTC's timing was strategically necessary. The agency effectively drew a line between 'structure/function claims' - which are legal and don't require pre-approval - and disease treatment claims, which require FDA drug approval. That distinction is the load-bearing wall of how supplements are regulated in the U.S., and in 2020 a significant number of companies were treating it like a suggestion.

What the Enforcement Action Got Right, and Where It Fell Short

The 2020 wave got a few things clearly right. It was fast. Warning letters went out within weeks of the most egregious claims appearing, which is unusually responsive for a federal agency. It was visible - the press coverage meant consumers at least had a chance to hear that regulators were paying attention. And it reinforced the substantiation standard in a way that put the broader industry on notice, not just the worst offenders. For more on this, see The FDA's 2023 Crackdown on NAD+ Supplements Was a Wake-Up Call the Industry Needed.

But here's where I'd push back on the triumphalism some people expressed about the crackdown... the enforcement was reactive, not structural. The fundamental problem in the supplement space is that the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 - DSHEA - put the burden on the FDA and FTC to prove harm or deception after the fact, rather than requiring companies to prove safety and efficacy before going to market. The FDA's own guidance makes clear that manufacturers are responsible for ensuring their products are safe and claims are truthful, but the pre-market bar is effectively low. Warning letters don't change that architecture.

In my work reviewing supplement research for athlete programs, I've seen this pattern repeat for years: enforcement spikes during a crisis, then fades when the news cycle moves on. The 2020 actions were meaningful within their scope. But if you're waiting for them to fundamentally clean up supplement marketing, you'll be waiting a while. The structural reform hasn't happened.

What 2020 Means for How You Should Read Supplement Labels Right Now

The practical takeaway from all of this isn't abstract. If you're buying supplements in 2020, or any time after, the 2020 FTC enforcement gives you a useful filter: any product claiming to treat, prevent, or mitigate a specific disease is almost certainly making an illegal claim, and you should treat that as a red flag about how that company thinks about evidence generally. The claim tells you something about the culture of the company, not just the product.

Structure/function claims are different and are legal under DSHEA when they meet the substantiation standard. 'Supports immune function' is legal if there's evidence for the ingredient at the dose used. 'Prevents COVID-19' is not legal, full stop. Learning to read that distinction on a label is genuinely useful. And when it comes to questions like timing your nutrients around those supplements, the same evidence-first logic applies - if you're interested in how nutrient timing fits into an actual performance protocol, understanding whether protein timing really matters as much as total intake is a good example of the kind of nuance that separates evidence-based practice from marketing-driven behavior.

And look, the immune-support category isn't uniformly bad. Some ingredients have real evidence behind them. Vitamin D deficiency is genuinely prevalent in northern latitudes during winter, and correcting a deficiency has documented immune implications. But 'correcting a deficiency' and 'megadosing for immune enhancement' are not the same thing, and most marketing in 2020 blurred that line deliberately. Your job as a consumer is to ask what specific claim is being made, at what dose, and what the evidence for that specific claim actually is. The FTC's 2020 actions didn't answer that question for you. They just punished the most obvious liars. The subtler ones are still on the shelf.

Before you buy any immune-support or wellness supplement, look up the specific ingredient at the specific dose listed on the label using PubMed or Examine.com, and ask whether the evidence cited is from healthy adults or a population very different from you. That one step will do more for your purchasing decisions than any regulatory crackdown.
