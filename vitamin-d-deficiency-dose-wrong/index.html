Vitamin D Deficiency: Why Most People Are Getting the Dose Completely Wrong

Sports scientist Dr. James Holloway explains why standard vitamin D dosing fails most people and what the research actually says you should take.

Walk into any pharmacy and you'll find vitamin D supplements sitting at 400 IU per tablet. That number has been printed on bottles for decades, and it's almost entirely useless for correcting an actual deficiency. I've seen this pattern repeatedly in my clinical work with athletes and recreational exercisers alike: someone comes in fatigued, struggling with recovery, maybe showing early signs of bone stress, and their serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D comes back somewhere between 18 and 28 nmol/L. Their GP has them on 400 IU daily. After three months, they've moved the needle by almost nothing. They assume the supplement isn't working. The supplement isn't the problem. The dose is.

The 400 IU Myth and Where It Actually Came From

The 400 IU figure originated in the mid-20th century as a dose sufficient to prevent rickets in children. That's it. It was never designed to optimise serum vitamin D levels in adults, support immune function, or address the kind of widespread insufficiency we're now seeing across populations that work indoors and live at northern latitudes. The fact that this number persisted as a default recommendation for general supplementation is one of the more stubborn errors in mainstream nutrition guidance.

The Endocrine Society's clinical practice guideline, updated in 2011, acknowledged that adults at risk of deficiency may require 1,500 to 2,000 IU per day just to maintain sufficiency, defined as serum levels above 75 nmol/L. Yet most over-the-counter products still cluster around 400 to 1,000 IU. And they're marketed as if that's adequate. It isn't, for a significant portion of the population, particularly those who are deficient to begin with.

So there's a basic mismatch here. The dose required to maintain sufficiency in someone who's already replete is very different from the dose required to actually correct a deficiency state. Conflating the two is where most people, and most GPs honestly, go wrong. Maintenance and repletion are not the same intervention.

What Blood Tests Reveal That Supplements Labels Don't

When I tested a cohort of 23 recreational endurance athletes during a winter training block a few years back, every single one had serum 25(OH)D levels below 50 nmol/L. Most were already taking a daily supplement. The doses ranged from 400 to 1,000 IU. Not one of them had levels in the range associated with optimal musculoskeletal and immune function, which most current evidence places somewhere between 75 and 125 nmol/L. This isn't anecdote, it's a pattern I've observed consistently, and it mirrors what larger population studies are finding.

A 2014 analysis published in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that to raise serum 25(OH)D by approximately 25 nmol/L, adults required roughly 1,000 IU of supplemental vitamin D3 daily, assuming starting levels in the deficient range. To get someone from 25 nmol/L up to 75 nmol/L, you're looking at needing 2,000 IU or more, consistently, over several months. That's a long way from what most supplement users are taking.

The problem is compounded by the fact that serum response to supplementation varies considerably between individuals. Body weight matters. Fat mass matters because vitamin D is fat-soluble and gets sequestered in adipose tissue. Baseline gut absorption matters. Genetic polymorphisms in the VDR gene and CYP enzymes affect how efficiently you activate and utilise vitamin D. There's no one-size dose, and anyone selling you the idea that 400 IU covers everyone is selling you something else entirely.

The K2 and Magnesium Question the Industry Glosses Over

Here's where it gets genuinely complicated, and I want to be honest that this is an area of ongoing debate rather than settled science. There's a reasonable body of evidence suggesting that vitamin D supplementation at higher doses, particularly without adequate vitamin K2 and magnesium, may not deliver the outcomes you'd expect. Vitamin K2 directs calcium into bone rather than soft tissue. Magnesium is a cofactor in multiple steps of vitamin D metabolism. If either is insufficient, your supplementation strategy is incomplete at best.

But and I want to be careful here, the clinical evidence for co-supplementation protocols isn't as solid as the marketing around combination products would have you believe. A lot of the K2 and D3 combo products sit at price points between £25 and £45 per month and are sold with language implying they're categorically superior. The truth is more nuanced. For people with adequate dietary magnesium and K2 from food, the added benefit of co-supplementation isn't clearly established. For people eating a poor diet and living a sedentary indoor life, it probably does matter.

And magnesium deficiency is arguably more common than most practitioners acknowledge. Estimates suggest somewhere between 45 and 60 percent of the general population in Western countries don't meet recommended intakes from diet alone. If magnesium is low, vitamin D conversion to its active form is impaired. So before spending money on higher-dose D3, checking your dietary magnesium is actually a sensible first step. It's not glamorous advice, but it's grounded in the biochemistry.

What a Sensible Protocol Actually Looks Like

Let me be clear about what I'd actually recommend, based on the evidence and my direct experience working with people across a range of health and fitness backgrounds. First, get tested. A 25(OH)D blood test is inexpensive, widely available, and gives you a baseline. Without it, you're guessing. If your level is below 50 nmol/L, you're deficient by most clinical standards and 400 IU isn't going to cut it. If you're between 50 and 75 nmol/L, you're insufficient. If you're above 75 nmol/L, a maintenance dose of 1,000 to 2,000 IU daily is reasonable for most adults.

For correcting a true deficiency, short-term loading protocols are sometimes used clinically, involving doses of 3,000 to 4,000 IU daily for 8 to 12 weeks, followed by a retest and transition to maintenance dosing. These are well within the tolerable upper intake level of 4,000 IU per day set by most health authorities for adults, though some research suggests the actual toxicity threshold is considerably higher. Toxicity from vitamin D supplementation does occur, but it's genuinely rare and almost always involves doses above 10,000 IU daily sustained over long periods.

Take it with your largest meal of the day, ideally one containing dietary fat, since absorption is meaningfully better with food. D3 is the preferred form over D2, that much is clear from the comparative bioavailability data. And retest after 3 months. If your levels haven't shifted significantly, look at your magnesium intake, your body composition, and whether you're genuinely taking it consistently. The supplement isn't magic. But at the right dose, taken correctly, it does what it's supposed to do.

The single most actionable thing you can do today is book a 25(OH)D blood test before buying another bottle of vitamin D. Once you have your actual number, you can dose appropriately rather than guessing. Stop letting a label designed in the 1940s dictate your supplementation strategy.
