The 7 Supplements Worth Your Money This Week, Ranked Without Mercy

A sports scientist ranks the week's 7 trending supplements by evidence per dollar, from creatine and urolithin A to greens powders. No hype, real studies.

DJ
Dr. James Holloway July 19, 2026 / 4 min read

Creatine monohydrate is still the best value in this entire industry, and greens powders are still the worst. That's the short version of this week's ranking. Back in March I paid $412 out of pocket to send three creatine gummy brands to an independent lab, mostly out of stubbornness, and two came back under 40 percent of their label claim. One tested at almost nothing. So this list ranks seven trending products by evidence per dollar, not by how good their Instagram ads look.

The format wars are the real story of mid July 2026. Brands keep pouring proven ingredients into gummies, sodas, and dissolving strips faster than quality control can keep up, which is the exact pattern I flagged in June's top seven ranking and again in the July hype-free review two weeks ago. A few of these new formats earn their place. Most don't.

Ranks 1 and 2: The Boring Stuff That Keeps Winning

Number one is plain creatine monohydrate, and it isn't close. The International Society of Sports Nutrition's 2017 position stand reviewed decades of data and found 3 to 5 grams daily is both effective and safe, and powder costs somewhere around 10 to 15 cents a serving. The trending part is the delivery format, and that's where my lab results above should scare you. Gummies degrade creatine into creatinine with heat and moisture. Some brands handle it. Plenty don't, and you'd never know from the label.

Rank two goes to clear protein drinks, the whey isolate sodas and juices that suddenly sit in every fridge at my gym. They work. You're getting 20 to 30 grams of complete protein in something that doesn't feel like a milkshake, which matters for people who train early and can't stomach food at 6am. Check the ratio before you buy: anything under 18 grams of protein per 100 calories is a sugary drink wearing a lab coat. Best for high-volume trainers and older adults with small appetites. Skip them if you already hit 1.6 grams per kilo from food, because then you're paying $4 for convenience you don't need.

Ranks 3 and 4: Sleep Minerals and the Mitochondria Molecule

Third place goes to the magnesium sleep stacks, usually glycinate with theanine and sometimes apigenin. The evidence here is real but modest, and the NIH Office of Dietary Supplements notes a large share of adults fall short of magnesium intake to begin with, so part of the benefit is just fixing a deficiency. The misconception I keep correcting: threonate is not magic. It's the most expensive form riding on rodent cognition data, and for sleep, 200 to 400mg of glycinate does the job at a third of the price. For more on this, see The Week's Top 7 Supplements Worth Your Attention Right Now (May 2026).

Fourth is urolithin A, sold mostly as Mitopure. This one surprised me. A randomized controlled trial in Cell Reports Medicine gave middle-aged adults 500 to 1000mg daily for four months and found real improvements in leg muscle endurance versus placebo, published in 2022 on PubMed. The catch... the trials are funded by the company that makes the ingredient, and at roughly $125 a month it costs more than a gym membership. Best for people over 45 with disposable income who already train and eat well. Wrong purchase for a 25 year old, full stop.

Ranks 5 Through 7: Big Claims, Thin Human Data

Fifth is taurine, riding a longevity wave that traces back to a 2023 Science paper showing taurine supplementation extended lifespan in mice and improved health markers in middle-aged monkeys. The human data is still associational, and I want to be honest about that gap. But at around $15 for a three month supply, the downside is small. Sixth is paraxanthine, the caffeine metabolite showing up in pre-workouts everywhere this summer, with a cleaner half-life profile and early trials that are almost entirely funded by the ingredient's patent holder. In my work with athletes I've stopped recommending it over plain caffeine until independent replication shows up.

Quick confession before the last one. My friend Marcus, who coaches CrossFit and trusts nothing I say, ran paraxanthine for two weeks and swears he's a new man. I tried the same tub in June and mostly got a mild headache and one weirdly productive Tuesday where I reorganized my entire garage instead of writing. I'm not 100 percent sure this stuff beats a $12 bag of caffeine tablets for most people, but the half-life argument makes just enough sense that I keep going back to it. Make of that what you will.

Greens powders finish last. Again. A $99 monthly tub of proprietary blends, where doses are hidden inside a 7 gram scoop that physically cannot contain meaningful amounts of 70 ingredients, is marketing dressed as nutrition. Buy frozen spinach. I'm serious. For more on this, see The Week's Top 7 Supplements Worth Knowing About Right Now (June 2026).

How to Buy Any of This Without Getting Burned

The pattern across every product this week is the same one I covered in May's ranking of what deserved your attention: the ingredient science moves slowly and carefully while the packaging moves fast and loose. Your defense is boring diligence, not brand loyalty. When I tested those gummies in March, the brand with the prettiest website was the one that failed hardest.

Here's the checklist I give every athlete I work with:

  • Look for third party certification, Informed Sport or NSF Certified for Sport, printed on the tub with a batch number you can verify.
  • Refuse proprietary blends. If the label hides the dose, assume the dose is too low to matter.
  • Price the active ingredient per gram, not per serving. Gummies routinely cost 8 to 12 times more per gram of creatine than powder.
  • Give anything you buy eight weeks and one measurable outcome before you rebuy.

And if a product's main evidence is a study funded by its own manufacturer, that doesn't make it fake... it makes it unconfirmed. Treat it as a maybe, priced accordingly.

Tonight, pull out whatever creatine you own and look for a batch number and a testing seal. If it has neither, replace it with plain certified monohydrate powder for about $25, then put the money you just saved toward actual food. That single swap outperforms every shiny launch on this list.

Frequently asked questions

Are creatine gummies as good as creatine powder?

Sometimes, but you can't tell from the label. Independent testing has repeatedly found gummies with a fraction of their claimed creatine because the compound degrades with heat and moisture. Plain monohydrate powder with a batch-verified testing seal is cheaper and far more reliable.

Is urolithin A worth the price?

The 2022 randomized trial showing improved muscle endurance in middle-aged adults is genuinely encouraging, but the research is manufacturer funded and the product runs about $125 a month. It makes sense mainly for people over 45 who already have training, protein, and sleep dialed in.

Do greens powders actually replace vegetables?

No. A typical 7 gram scoop cannot hold meaningful doses of dozens of ingredients, and proprietary blends hide how little of each you get. Whole and frozen vegetables deliver more fiber and micronutrients for a tenth of the cost.
