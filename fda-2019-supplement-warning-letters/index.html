The FDA's 2019 Warning Letter Surge: What It Really Meant for the Supplement Industry

The FDA issued a record number of supplement warning letters in 2019. Here's what it meant for the industry and what you should have taken from it.

DJ
Dr. James Holloway October 20, 2019 / 5 min read

What the FDA Actually Did in 2019 and Why It Was Different This Time

In April 2019, FDA Commissioner Scott Gottlieb announced a broad new strategy for supplement oversight, describing what he called a "new era of smarter food policy" that would directly target the supplement sector. The agency issued warning letters to 12 companies in a single day, an unprecedented coordinated action. The letters cited illegal disease claims, products spiked with pharmaceutical ingredients including hidden stimulants, and labelling that was plainly fraudulent.

This mattered because the FDA had historically moved slowly on supplements. The Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 had placed the burden of proof on the agency to demonstrate a product was unsafe before pulling it, rather than requiring companies to prove safety before selling. For 25 years, that legal framework had been a shield for bad actors. The 2019 letters didn't change the law, but they signalled a shift in how aggressively the agency intended to use the enforcement tools it already had.

Specifically, the agency focused on two categories: products making explicit or implied disease claims (which legally require drug approval), and products found to contain undisclosed active pharmaceutical ingredients. On the latter point, the numbers were alarming. The FDA's own tainted products database had grown to include hundreds of entries, predominantly weight loss, sexual enhancement, and bodybuilding products containing compounds like sildenafil, sibutramine, and anabolic steroids, none of which belonged anywhere near a supplement label.

The Adulterant Problem Was Worse Than Most People Realised

Here's where things get genuinely troubling. A 2018 study published in JAMA Internal Medicine analysed 776 dietary supplements that had been recalled or flagged by the FDA between 2007 and 2016, and found that 67% of those products, when retested up to four years later, still contained the same undisclosed drug ingredients. In other words, warning letters alone had historically achieved almost nothing. Companies would quietly reformulate or rebrand, then continue selling essentially the same product.

In my work with competitive athletes, I've seen this pattern play out repeatedly. A client comes in having tested positive for a banned substance, insisting they never knowingly took a drug. In most cases, they're telling the truth. The contamination is real, and it's not always accidental. Some manufacturers deliberately spike products because the pharmaceutical compound delivers the effect users want, which creates repeat customers. It's cynical, it's dangerous, and the 2019 enforcement action was one of the few moments the regulator actually named names publicly.

The practical implication for consumers and practitioners in 2019 was clear: third-party testing certifications weren't a nice-to-have, they were essential. Programmes like NSF Certified for Sport and Informed Sport existed precisely because regulatory oversight had been insufficient. If anything, the FDA's public action in 2019 validated what many of us had been saying for years.

Industry Reaction: Largely Defensive, Occasionally Constructive

The supplement industry's trade body, the Council for Responsible Nutrition, issued a statement broadly welcoming stricter enforcement while pushing back on what they characterised as overly broad application of disease-claim rules. Their concern wasn't entirely without merit. The line between a structure-function claim (permitted) and a disease claim (requires drug approval) can be genuinely ambiguous, and some smaller manufacturers had made innocent errors rather than deliberate misrepresentations. That nuance deserves acknowledgement.

But honestly, a lot of the industry reaction felt like motivated defensiveness. Okay, I'll admit something a bit embarrassing here: I actually attended a trade webinar in June 2019 where a supplement company's regulatory consultant spent 40 minutes explaining why their client's product, which literally said "clinically proven to reduce blood glucose," was technically not a disease claim. My friend who runs a gym supply shop in Bristol listened to the same session and texted me halfway through: "This is just an expensive man explaining why the rules don't apply to him." He wasn't wrong. I laughed so hard I dropped my coffee. The sophistication of the rationalisation was inversely proportional to its credibility. For more on this, see The FDA's 2023 Crackdown on NAD+ Supplements Was a Wake-Up Call the Industry Needed.

The more constructive responses came from larger, established brands that had already invested in quality manufacturing and testing infrastructure. For them, stricter enforcement was actually welcome because it levelled a playing field that had been tilted toward cheap, unscrupulous competitors. Enforcement doesn't hurt responsible manufacturers. It exposes irresponsible ones.

What This Means for How You Should Buy and Recommend Supplements Now

The 2019 action was a warning shot, and the follow-through would matter as much as the initial move. Those of us watching the space knew that without sustained enforcement, the industry would revert to form. And that concern proved prescient: the broader regulatory picture remained patchy heading into 2020. The FTC's 2020 crackdown on supplement advertising claims picked up some of the slack, addressing the marketing side of what the FDA had started addressing on the manufacturing and labelling side. But two agencies playing whack-a-mole with a $40-plus billion industry is not a substitute for structural reform.

For practitioners, the 2019 events reinforced what the evidence had long suggested: the category of supplement matters enormously when assessing risk. Protein powders and creatine monohydrate exist in a very different risk environment than proprietary blends marketed for weight loss or "testosterone support." When you're thinking about something like protein supplementation, the practical question of when you eat protein and whether timing actually matters as much as total intake is the kind of evidence-based debate worth having, far removed from the regulatory horror show of adulterated thermogenics.

There are a few practical filters worth applying to any supplement purchase in this environment:

  • Third-party certification: NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport, or USP verification. Non-negotiable for athletes subject to testing.
  • Claim language: If it sounds like a drug claim, it probably is one. Avoid products that promise to "treat," "cure," or "prevent" anything.
  • Proprietary blends: These mask individual ingredient doses. If you can't see what's in it and at what dose, you can't evaluate it.
  • Company transparency: Reputable brands publish third-party certificates of analysis. If they won't, ask why not.

I'm not 100% sure this framework works for every consumer in every situation, but at least in my experience, applying these filters eliminates the vast majority of genuinely risky products before you even have to evaluate the efficacy question.
