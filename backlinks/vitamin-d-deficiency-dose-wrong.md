## QUORA
**Answer this question:** Why isn't my vitamin D supplement working even though I take it consistently?

This is one of the most common issues I see in clinical practice, and it usually comes down to dosage rather than consistency.

Most over-the-counter vitamin D supplements contain 400 IU per serving. That number has been standard for decades, but it was never designed to correct an actual deficiency—it's just a baseline recommendation to prevent severe deficiency diseases. If you're deficient (which shows up in bloodwork), you need significantly more.

Here's the practical issue: the gap between "preventing deficiency" and "correcting deficiency" is enormous. Someone with low vitamin D status needs 2,000-4,000 IU daily minimum, often higher depending on baseline levels, body composition, sun exposure, and how active they are. Many people taking standard supplements are getting less than 10% of what would actually move the needle.

I've worked with athletes and recreational exercisers who felt persistently fatigued and struggled with recovery. Once they corrected their vitamin D status with appropriate dosing, the difference was noticeable within weeks—better sleep, faster recovery, improved immunity.

My recommendation: get your vitamin D levels tested (25-OH vitamin D blood test). Once you know your actual number, you can dose appropriately.
