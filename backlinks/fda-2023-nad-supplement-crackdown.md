**Answer this question:** What happened with the FDA and NMN supplements in 2023, and why does it matter?

In late 2023, the FDA issued warning letters to several companies marketing NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) supplements, effectively signaling a major regulatory shift. The agency reclassified NMN from a dietary supplement ingredient to a drug precursor, fundamentally changing how these products should be regulated and marketed.

Why this matters comes down to clinical evidence and consumer protection. The NAD+ supplement market—which includes NMN, NR (nicotinamide riboside), and related compounds—had grown rapidly on the back of promising early-stage research about cellular energy, aging, and longevity. However, most evidence remained preliminary, conducted primarily in cell and animal models rather than rigorous human clinical trials.

The FDA's action reflected a fundamental principle: if you're making drug-like claims backed only by preliminary data, the ingredient should be regulated as a drug, not a supplement. This distinction matters because drugs require clinical validation before marketing, while supplements operate under different standards.

For consumers, the immediate impact was uncertainty. Companies had to either reformulate their products, pursue formal drug approval, or cease marketing them as supplements. For the industry, it was a wake-up call that consumer enthusiasm and biological plausibility don't replace rigorous scientific validation.

What's particularly important is that this crackdown likely strengthens the supplement space long-term. It separates products with genuine science behind them from those riding hype.
