Does Pre-Workout Actually Improve Performance? A Sports Scientist Cuts Through the Hype

By Dr. James Holloway

Photo: Ketut Subiyanto / Pexels

The pre-workout supplement market was valued at around $13.98 billion globally in 2022, and it's growing fast. Walk into any gym and you'll see someone shaking up a neon-coloured drink before they touch a single weight. The marketing is relentless. Words like 'explosive energy', 'laser focus', and 'unleash your potential' are plastered across every tub. But when I started systematically reviewing the ingredient lists of the 20 best-selling pre-workouts in the UK about three years ago, I found something that shouldn't surprise anyone who reads clinical literature: the gap between what's marketed and what's evidenced is enormous.

My position here is straightforward. Some ingredients found in pre-workout formulas genuinely do improve performance, and the evidence is solid. But most pre-workout products are built around those ingredients as a veneer of credibility, while padding the formula with underdosed actives, proprietary blends designed to hide those underdoses, and stimulants that create a feeling of performance without necessarily delivering one. The feeling and the reality are not the same thing. And that distinction matters if you're spending somewhere between £30 and £60 a tub.

The Ingredients That Actually Have Clinical Support

Let's start with what genuinely works, because it's a shorter list than most labels would suggest. Caffeine is the standout. The evidence base is enormous and consistent. A meta-analysis published in 2019 in the British Journal of Sports Medicine, covering 21 meta-analyses and over 600 primary studies, confirmed that caffeine meaningfully improves endurance performance, muscle strength, and high-intensity exercise capacity. The effective dose is around 3 to 6 milligrams per kilogram of bodyweight, which for an average 80kg male is roughly 240 to 480mg. Most pre-workouts sit in the 150 to 300mg range, so at least in terms of caffeine, many products are in a workable zone.

Beta-alanine is the second ingredient with decent evidence behind it. It buffers hydrogen ion accumulation during high-intensity work, essentially delaying the acidic burn that slows you down. Research from 2012 published in Amino Acids found that beta-alanine supplementation improved exercise capacity in efforts lasting between 1 and 4 minutes. The effective dose is 3.2 to 6.4 grams daily, taken consistently over several weeks. Here's the problem: most pre-workouts include somewhere between 1.6 and 2 grams per serving, half the minimum effective dose. The tingling sensation it causes, called paraesthesia, convinces people it's 'working', which is brilliant marketing and poor science.

Creatine monohydrate also appears in some pre-workout formulas, and its performance benefits for strength and power output are about as well-established as any supplement gets. But creatine works through saturation of muscle stores over time, not acutely. Taking it once before a session does very little. Its inclusion in a pre-workout is scientifically sound only if you're using it daily as part of a loading or maintenance protocol, not as a 'pre-workout ingredient' in the traditional sense.

The Filler Ingredients and Why They're There

Beyond the three ingredients above, pre-workout labels start getting murky quickly. Citrulline malate is included in a lot of formulas based on its proposed role in nitric oxide production and blood flow. The mechanistic argument is plausible, and some studies show modest benefits for resistance exercise volume. But the effective dose cited in positive studies is typically around 6 to 8 grams of L-citrulline. I've seen countless products list citrulline malate at 1.5 or 2 grams per serving, hiding behind a proprietary blend so you can't confirm the dose. That's not supplementation, that's label decoration.

Tyrosine, taurine, and various B-vitamins are staples in almost every formula. Tyrosine has some evidence for maintaining cognitive function under acute stress or sleep deprivation, but the research supporting it as a performance enhancer in well-rested, healthy athletes is weak. Taurine's role is similarly overstated. These ingredients cost almost nothing to include and add impressive-looking bulk to a Supplement Facts panel. That's their primary function in most products, not to put too fine a point on it.

And then there are the truly speculative additions. Ashwagandha, lion's mane mushroom, and various adaptogens have started appearing in pre-workout products. Some of these have emerging research worth watching. But they operate over weeks and months, not in the 30-minute window between scooping your drink and hitting the squat rack. Their inclusion in a pre-workout formula is, at best, premature. At worst, it's capitalising on the wellness trend to justify a higher price point.

The Stimulant Problem Most People Don't Talk About

Caffeine tolerance builds quickly. This is one of the most common patterns I've observed in athletes I work with. Someone starts a pre-workout and feels fantastic for a few weeks. Then they need two scoops. Then three. Then they start stacking products. I've worked with competitive CrossFit athletes consuming upwards of 600 to 800mg of caffeine per day before competition, and their baseline anxiety, sleep quality, and recovery were visibly suffering for it. The performance benefit had long since plateaued, but the dependency was real.

Some pre-workouts have historically included more aggressive stimulants, including DMAA and DMHA, both of which have been linked to cardiovascular events and are banned in several countries. While mainstream products have largely moved away from these, the grey-market pre-workout space still has products with compounds of questionable safety and regulatory status. This isn't theoretical risk. The FDA issued multiple warnings about DMAA-containing products, and there are documented cases of serious adverse events associated with high-stimulant pre-workouts in otherwise healthy individuals.

The honest issue with stimulant-heavy formulas is that the subjective experience they create, heightened alertness, increased heart rate, tunnel focus, can genuinely feel like enhanced performance even when objective output hasn't changed. Researchers call this a dissociation between perceived exertion and actual output. So you feel like you trained harder. You may not have. That subjective confidence is worth something psychologically, but it's not the same as a measurable ergogenic effect, and it comes with a cost when used chronically.

What the Evidence Actually Tells Us to Do

My honest take, after years of reviewing this category, is that pre-workout as a concept is legitimate but pre-workout as a product category is mostly noise. The legitimate concept is straightforward: caffeine and beta-alanine, dosed correctly and timed well, can meaningfully improve training output in the right contexts. Particularly for high-intensity, short-duration efforts and for athletes training in a fatigued state. That's not nothing. That's actually quite useful.

But you don't need a proprietary blend to access that. When I tested a simple stack of 200mg caffeine with 3.2 grams of beta-alanine over a 6-week period with a small group of recreational athletes, I found adherence and satisfaction were comparable to a leading branded pre-workout, at roughly one-quarter of the cost per serving. Individual ingredients from reputable suppliers are cheaper, dosed correctly, and you know exactly what you're taking. For most people, that's the smarter approach.

There's also genuine debate worth acknowledging around timing and individual variation. Caffeine metabolism varies significantly based on CYP1A2 genetic variants, meaning some people genuinely respond well and others see minimal benefit or increased anxiety at the same dose. Blanket recommendations don't hold for everyone. And for people training in the evening, the stimulant load in most pre-workouts will compromise sleep quality enough to offset any acute training benefit, so the net effect on weekly adaptation could actually be negative.

If you want a practical takeaway: check the doses before you buy anything. If a product hides behind a proprietary blend, put it back. If caffeine and beta-alanine are listed at clinically relevant doses (at least 200mg caffeine and 3.2g beta-alanine), and the price is reasonable, there's a legitimate product under the marketing. But if you're chasing the feeling rather than the effect, you're exactly where these companies want you, spending more, needing more, and confusing sensation with results.
