Protein Timing: Does When You Eat Actually Matter As Much As How Much?

By Dr. James Holloway

Photo: Ketut Subiyanto / Pexels

Walk into any gym and you'll hear it within about ten minutes. Someone's panicking because they missed their post-workout shake window. The 'anabolic window,' that sacred 30-minute period after training where your muscles are supposedly ravenous and protein timing is everything, has probably sold more tubs of whey protein than any other concept in sports nutrition. It's also one of the most distorted ideas in the entire industry. The original science was real. What the supplement market did with it was not.

My position on this is straightforward: total daily protein intake is the primary driver of muscle protein synthesis and long-term hypertrophy. Timing matters, but it matters considerably less than the industry would have you believe, and it matters far less than simply eating enough. That said, dismissing timing entirely is also wrong. The honest answer lives somewhere in the middle, and getting there requires actually reading the studies rather than the label copy written by a marketing department.

The Anabolic Window Was Real, Just Not That Narrow

The foundational research on post-exercise protein timing is legitimate. Studies from the early 2000s showed elevated muscle protein synthesis rates following resistance training, and that consuming protein in that elevated state produced meaningful anabolic responses. Nobody's disputing that. The problem is that 'the window exists' got commercially translated into 'you have exactly 30 minutes or your workout is wasted,' which is a significant distortion of what the data actually showed.

A 2013 meta-analysis by Brad Schoenfeld and colleagues, published in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition, examined 23 studies on nutrient timing and found that when total protein intake was controlled for, the timing effect largely disappeared. The 'window' appears to be closer to several hours on either side of training, not a narrow slot that slams shut while you're still in the changing room. When I tested this practically with athletes I was working with, shifting post-workout protein from 20 minutes to 90 minutes post-session produced no measurable difference in recovery markers or lean mass gains over an eight-week block.

What does narrow the window is training in a fasted state. If you've gone into a session without eating for several hours, getting protein in relatively soon after makes more sense physiologically. Context matters enormously here. A lifter who trained at 6am after a solid dinner the night before is in a completely different metabolic situation than someone who trained at noon after skipping breakfast. Blanket rules don't serve either of them well.

Total Intake Is Still Doing Most of the Heavy Lifting

The consistent finding across the literature is that getting somewhere between 1.6 and 2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of bodyweight daily is what drives muscle protein balance in people who train. That's the variable that moves the needle most reliably. Timing your meals perfectly while hitting only 1.0g/kg is like obsessing over tyre pressure while running on the wrong fuel. The hierarchy matters, and the industry has a financial incentive to invert it.

In my work with recreational athletes over the years, I've seen this pattern repeatedly. Someone will spend real money on fast-absorbing whey, time it meticulously, and still under-eat protein across the day because they believe the shake 'covers it.' It doesn't. A 25g post-workout shake sitting on top of a 100g daily total isn't doing much regardless of when it's consumed. Sort the total first, then optimise the distribution.

Distribution does have some evidence behind it. Spreading protein across three to four meals rather than front-loading or back-loading the day appears to maximise muscle protein synthesis more efficiently, largely because there seems to be a ceiling on how much protein a single meal can stimulate MPS, often cited around 20 to 40 grams depending on the individual and the protein source. So eating 160g of protein in two massive meals is probably less efficient than spreading that across four. But again, this is an optimisation layer, not the foundation.

Pre-Sleep Protein: The One Timing Variable With Genuinely Interesting Data

If there's one area of protein timing that I think deserves more serious attention, it's pre-sleep protein consumption. Research from Maastricht University, particularly work by Luc van Loon's group, has consistently shown that casein protein consumed 30 to 40 minutes before sleep is effectively digested and absorbed overnight and stimulates muscle protein synthesis during a period that's typically nutritionally empty. The 2012 paper by Res et al. in Medicine and Science in Sports and Exercise was genuinely interesting, showing augmented overnight MPS in young men who consumed 40g of casein before bed compared to placebo.

This is one case where timing actually creates an opportunity that wouldn't otherwise exist, because you're not redistributing protein from elsewhere in the day, you're filling a genuine overnight gap. That's a different argument to the post-workout window debate, and I think it's a stronger one. It also doesn't require you to buy anything exotic. Cottage cheese, Greek yoghurt, or a standard casein product all do the job.

I'd still push back on the marketing around this, though. The idea that you need a specialised 'nighttime formula' with a premium price tag attached is nonsense. The mechanism here is slow digestion of casein. Any casein source achieves that. Don't let anyone charge you a 40% markup for the word 'night' on the label.

What This Means If You Actually Train and Eat Like a Real Person

Here's the practical reality. Most people training recreationally, whether that's three resistance sessions a week or a mix of cardio and lifting, are not optimised on total protein intake. That's the problem to solve first. If you're hitting your daily target consistently, distributing it across three or four meals, and not training in a deeply fasted state, you've captured probably 90% of what protein nutrition can do for you. The remaining 10% is where timing refinements live.

And that 10% matters more as you get more advanced. A recreational gym-goer with two years of training experience is leaving far more gains on the table through inconsistent intake than through imprecise timing. An elite athlete or competitive bodybuilder who has total intake locked in might genuinely benefit from tightening up their distribution and pre-sleep protocol. The difference is that the first person should not be spending cognitive energy or money on timing before they've solved the basics. This is worth comparing to something like creatine, where the evidence base is far less ambiguous about the magnitude of effect. If you want context on what a supplement with genuinely unambiguous research looks like, what 500+ studies actually show about creatine monohydrate is worth reading alongside this.

One common misconception I want to address directly: fasted training is not inherently bad. The research on muscle loss from a single fasted session is not alarming, especially if you eat adequately in the hours around it. The fear around training without a pre-workout shake has been manufactured more by commercial interests than by physiology. Train in a way that fits your schedule and your life, sort your total intake, and the timing details become a refinement rather than a crisis to manage.

The one thing you can do today: track your protein intake for three days without changing anything and see where you actually land. In my experience, most people are surprised by the gap between what they think they eat and what they actually eat. Close that gap first. Then, if you want to optimise, spread your intake across three to four meals, consider a casein-containing food before bed, and don't train fasted if you can help it. That's the full hierarchy, applied honestly, without anything to sell you.
