Most Probiotics Do Nothing. These Few Strains Actually Earn Their Price

A sports scientist breaks down which probiotic strains have real clinical trials behind them for gut health, and which ones are marketing.

DJ
Dr. James Holloway July 17, 2026 / 3 min read

Most probiotics on the shelf will do nothing measurable for your gut, and the handful that work do so only for specific problems tied to specific named strains. Back in 2019 I ran an informal test on myself after a brutal course of antibiotics wrecked my digestion for a fortnight. I bought a 40 billion CFU bottle from a big high-street brand, took it religiously, and felt exactly the same. Then a gastroenterologist I was working with told me to try Saccharomyces boulardii instead, and within about four days the difference was obvious. That gap between a random blend and a trialled strain is the whole story here.

Here's my position, and I'll defend it: the word "probiotic" on a label tells you almost nothing. What matters is the exact strain, the dose, and whether that precise combination has been through human trials for the thing you actually want it to fix. Genus, species, strain. Skip any one of those three and you're guessing.

The strain is the drug, not the label

A probiotic is defined by three parts. Genus (Lactobacillus), species (rhamnosus), and strain (GG). That last bit, the strain code, is where the evidence lives. Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG behaves nothing like Lactobacillus rhamnosus HN001, even though they share a species name. It's a bit like saying two people are the same because they share a surname.

So when a bottle just says "contains Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium," that's the equivalent of a supplement that says "contains vitamins." You have no idea what you're getting or whether it's been tested. The NIH Office of Dietary Supplements makes this point flatly: health effects are strain-specific and cannot be assumed to carry across even closely related strains.

In my work reviewing supplement dossiers, the single fastest way to spot a weak product is to look for the strain designation. If it's missing, the manufacturer either doesn't know or doesn't want you to. Both are bad signs.

The strains that actually have trials behind them

A short, honest list. These have repeatable human evidence for defined uses, not vague "gut wellness" claims.

Saccharomyces boulardii for antibiotic-associated diarrhoea. This is a beneficial yeast, not a bacterium, so antibiotics can't kill it. A large Cochrane review pooling thousands of patients found probiotics, S. boulardii among the best studied, cut the risk of C. difficile-associated diarrhoea by around 60 percent in people on antibiotics. That's a real number from real trials.

Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG for acute infectious diarrhoea, especially in kids. Decades of paediatric data. It shortens the illness by roughly a day in most cases, which matters a lot when you've got a sick toddler.

Bifidobacterium longum subsp. infantis 35624 for IBS symptoms. The 2005 trial published in Gastroenterology by O'Mahony and colleagues showed it eased abdominal pain and bloating better than a Lactobacillus comparator and placebo. It's sold as Align in a lot of markets.

The De Simone formulation (once sold as VSL#3) for pouchitis and as an add-on in ulcerative colitis. High-dose, eight-strain, and genuinely one of the few blends with clinic-grade backing.

Where the marketing outruns the science

Now the part the industry hates. There is no good evidence that a daily probiotic "boosts immunity," "detoxes" your gut, or improves general health in people who are already well. A widely discussed 2018 study in Cell from the Weizmann Institute found that in many healthy adults, the ingested strains just passed straight through and never colonised the gut lining at all. The bugs went in one end and out the other, doing very little in between.

And here's the thing... colony forming unit counts are marketing theatre most of the time. A bottle screaming "100 billion CFU" isn't better than a trialled 10 billion product if those 100 billion are untested strains. Higher number, prettier label, same nothing.

The "soil-based" and "whole biome" products lean hardest on vibes. Some may turn out useful. Right now the trial data is thin, so I treat the confident claims as sales copy, not science.

How to actually choose one without getting fleeced

Match the strain to your specific problem, then check that strain has been trialled for that problem. Not "gut health" in the abstract. A concrete complaint.

My rough rules, and I'll admit they're rules of thumb, not gospel:

  • Going on antibiotics? S. boulardii, started the same day, continued a week or two after.
  • IBS with bloating and pain? Try B. infantis 35624 for at least four weeks before you judge it.
  • Otherwise healthy and just want "gut support"? Save your money and eat more fibre and fermented food. Kefir, kimchi, live yoghurt.

My mate Tom, who is about as far from a supplement nerd as you can get, once asked me why his 90 quid "advanced biome" subscription wasn't doing anything, and honestly I didn't have the heart to tell him straight away. Turned out the label listed no strain codes at all, just species names and a big CFU number. He'd basically been paying a premium for expensive yoghurt in capsule form. He switched to actual kefir and reckons he feels better, though I'd gently point out that could just as easily be him eating better overall. Hard to untangle. That's the messy truth of self-experiments.

One honest caveat: response is individual. In my experience testing these across different people, the same trialled strain that transforms one person's symptoms barely touches another's. The trials show average effects. You are not an average.

Do this today: pick up whatever probiotic you own and look for the full strain code, the three-part name like Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG or Bifidobacterium longum 35624. If it's not printed on the label, you're holding a guess, and you should either replace it with a strain that's been trialled for your specific issue or stop buying it and put the money toward real fermented food.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to take probiotics every day for them to work?

Only while you're targeting a specific issue. For antibiotic-associated diarrhoea you take S. boulardii through the course and a week or two after. For IBS, most trials run four weeks or more. There's little evidence that healthy people benefit from taking them indefinitely.

Are more CFUs always better in a probiotic?

No. The CFU count only matters within a strain that's actually been trialled at that dose. A 100 billion CFU bottle of untested strains is not better than a 10 billion product with clinical backing for your specific problem.

Can I just eat yoghurt or kefir instead of taking capsules?

For general gut support, often yes. Fermented foods like kefir, live yoghurt and kimchi deliver live cultures plus fibre. But for a defined medical use, like preventing C. difficile diarrhoea, you want the specific trialled strain at the trialled dose, which food can't guarantee.
