Creatine Monohydrate: What 500+ Studies Actually Show (And What the Industry Won't Tell You)

By Dr. James Holloway

Photo: Jonathan Borba / Pexels

Creatine monohydrate has been studied more rigorously than almost any other supplement in sports science. We're talking over 500 peer-reviewed trials across more than three decades. And yet, walk into any supplement store and you'll still find it buried under newer, flashier products with half the evidence and three times the price tag. That tells you something about how this industry operates. The science on creatine isn't complicated or contested. It works. The question worth asking is: for what, for whom, and under what conditions?

My position here is simple: creatine monohydrate is the single most evidence-backed performance supplement available to recreational and elite athletes alike, and the decades of marketing spin around it, loading phases, proprietary blends, 'enhanced' delivery systems, have done more to confuse consumers than help them. This article isn't a product review. It's a science review. I want to walk through what the evidence actually supports, where the genuine uncertainties lie, and why the basic, cheap, unflavoured powder is almost certainly all you need.

The Phosphocreatine Mechanism: Why This Supplement Actually Earns Its Place

Creatine works by increasing phosphocreatine stores in skeletal muscle, which accelerates ATP resynthesis during high-intensity, short-duration efforts. Sprinting, heavy lifting, repeated jumping, these are the contexts where creatine supplementation consistently delivers measurable gains. A meta-analysis published in 2003 by Branch in the International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism pooled data from 22 studies and found average strength increases of around 8% and power output improvements of roughly 14% compared to placebo. Those aren't trivial numbers.

What that mechanism also tells you is what creatine won't do. Endurance performance in events lasting longer than about 90 seconds shows minimal benefit in most trials. The ATP-PCr system simply isn't the dominant energy pathway during a marathon or a 10k row. I've seen this pattern repeatedly in my work with competitive cyclists and triathletes who'd been sold creatine as a general performance enhancer. Their power during sprint intervals improved, their sustained aerobic output didn't shift meaningfully.

So the mechanism is specific, and that specificity is actually a strength of the evidence base. We know why it works, we know where it works, and that predictability is something you rarely get with most sports supplements.

Loading Protocols, Dosing, and the Marketing Noise Around Both

Here's where the industry loves to overcomplicate things. The classic loading protocol, 20g per day split into four doses for five to seven days followed by a maintenance dose of 3 to 5g daily, does saturate muscle creatine stores faster. That's true. But here's what the supplement companies conveniently omit: you reach the same saturation endpoint with 3 to 5g daily after around 28 days. The loading phase is optional, not essential.

When I tested both protocols with a group of strength athletes during a research project several years ago, the performance differences at the eight-week mark were negligible. The loading group got there faster, but they also reported more gastrointestinal discomfort early on and spent more on product in the first week. For someone competing in two weeks, loading makes sense. For someone building a year-round supplement routine, it's largely unnecessary.

The timing debate, pre-workout versus post-workout, is genuinely less settled than either camp claims. Some data suggests post-exercise creatine uptake is slightly superior, possibly due to improved insulin sensitivity in trained muscle. But the effect size is small enough that consistency matters far more than timing. Take it when you'll actually remember to take it.

Beyond Muscle: The Cognitive and Clinical Evidence That Gets Ignored

Most coverage of creatine focuses exclusively on muscle performance, which is understandable but increasingly incomplete. There's a growing body of evidence looking at creatine's role in brain energy metabolism, and some of it is genuinely interesting, though I'd urge caution before anyone starts calling it a nootropic. The brain relies on phosphocreatine buffering too, particularly under conditions of mental fatigue, sleep deprivation, or hypoxic stress.

A randomised controlled trial from 2002 by Rae and colleagues found that oral creatine supplementation of 5g daily for six weeks produced significant improvements in working memory and processing speed in young adult vegetarians. The vegetarian population matters here because dietary creatine comes almost exclusively from meat and fish, so baseline muscle and brain creatine stores in vegetarians tend to be lower. The effect sizes in omnivores are generally smaller, which is worth keeping in mind if you're reading headlines that suggest creatine will make everyone measurably smarter.

There's also credible research in clinical populations, including older adults with sarcopenia, patients undergoing chemotherapy, and individuals with certain muscular dystrophies. None of this means creatine is a therapeutic drug. But it does mean the conversation has moved well past gym performance, and the evidence base supports that expansion.

Safety Profile, Non-Responders, and the One Genuine Debate Worth Having

Let's address the kidney concern directly, because it still circulates online despite being thoroughly examined. In healthy individuals with normal renal function, creatine supplementation at standard doses does not damage kidneys. Creatinine, a metabolic byproduct of creatine, will be elevated in blood tests during supplementation, which can look alarming if a clinician doesn't know you're supplementing. But elevated creatinine in this context is not evidence of kidney damage. Studies tracking long-term users over five or more years have found no adverse renal markers.

The one area of genuine scientific debate is non-responders. Somewhere between 25 and 30% of individuals show minimal or no increase in muscle phosphocreatine following supplementation. This is real, it's not manufacturer spin, and the likely explanation involves baseline dietary creatine intake and pre-existing muscle creatine saturation. If you've been eating red meat daily for years, your muscles may already be close to saturated. But it could also reflect genuine individual variation in creatine transporter expression. We don't fully understand this yet.

What we do know is that creatine monohydrate, the plain, micronised powder you can buy for around £15 to £20 for a kilogram, performs identically to 'Kre-Alkalyn', 'buffered creatine', 'creatine HCl', and every other patented variant that costs two to four times as much. The bioavailability data doesn't support the premium. And that, in my view, is the most important thing the evidence base tells us: the cheapest version is the one with the most research behind it.

If you're training seriously and you're not supplementing with creatine monohydrate, you're probably leaving a meaningful, well-evidenced performance benefit on the table. Start with 3 to 5g daily, skip the loading phase unless you have a specific short-term competition window, and buy the unflavoured generic powder rather than any branded variant. That's it. The science here isn't ambiguous, and it doesn't require a premium product to act on.
