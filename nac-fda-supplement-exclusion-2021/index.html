NAC Got Pulled From Amazon in 2021. The FDA's Logic Still Doesn't Hold Up.

The FDA's 2021 move to exclude NAC from dietary supplements shook the industry. Here's what actually happened and why it still matters.

DJ
Dr. James Holloway February 11, 2021 / 6 min read

In the spring of 2021, Amazon quietly began pulling N-acetyl cysteine products from its platform, and for a few weeks the supplement world genuinely didn't know what to hit back with. I'd been recommending NAC to endurance athletes in my practice for years at that point, mostly for its role in replenishing glutathione after heavy oxidative stress from back-to-back training blocks. I remember one client, a masters-level cyclist who'd been using 600 mg daily for about eight months, calling me in a flat panic because his usual tub had vanished from his cart. He wasn't alone. Tens of thousands of Amazon listings disappeared almost overnight, with no clear regulatory timeline attached. The trigger was a formal FDA statement arguing that NAC couldn't legally be sold as a dietary supplement because it had been studied as a drug, specifically as the prescription treatment Mucomyst, before it was ever marketed as a supplement. That argument sounds tidy on paper. It really doesn't hold up under scrutiny.

The FDA's position rested on a provision in the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994, which bars ingredients from supplement status if they were first marketed as an approved drug. The agency had actually received two citizen petitions on this back in 2020, and by early 2021 it acknowledged those petitions publicly without giving a definitive answer either way. What followed was a regulatory grey zone that cost small brands real money, confused consumers who were using NAC for legitimate reasons like liver support and respiratory health, and exposed just how much damage an inconclusive FDA statement can do when Amazon decides to act on it preemptively. The industry's response was loud, fragmented, and ultimately instructive.

What the FDA Actually Said, and What It Left Out

The agency never issued a formal rule. What it did was respond to two citizen petitions, one filed by the Natural Products Association and one by a coalition of industry lawyers, by acknowledging that NAC might be excluded under the drug-preclusion clause of DSHEA. That's a long way from a final determination. But Amazon, predictably, interpreted ambiguity as liability and acted on the side of caution. By April 2021, Natural Products Insider was reporting that hundreds of NAC products had been removed or suppressed from search results, covering brands ranging from NOW Foods to Jarrow Formulas.

The drug-preclusion argument itself has a factual problem. NAC has been sold as a dietary supplement in the United States since at least the early 1990s, and Mucomyst received its FDA approval in 1963. Whether NAC was "marketed as a dietary supplement" before that drug approval is genuinely debatable, and the FDA's own guidance on drug preclusion has historically focused on ingredients first introduced to the market as drugs, not ingredients with a long, parallel history in the supplement space. The agency essentially chose to apply maximum ambiguity to a molecule with a safety record that's about as clean as any ingredient in sports nutrition.

For context on how the FDA handles grey-area enforcement, the pattern isn't new. The 2019 wave of warning letters, which you can read about in detail in this breakdown of the FDA's 2019 warning letter surge, showed the same tendency to create regulatory pressure without clean resolution. Brands are left guessing, and the ones with the least legal resource lose first.

The Science Behind NAC That Amazon's Removal Ignored

Let's be clear about what NAC does. It's a precursor to glutathione, the body's primary endogenous antioxidant. In clinical settings it's used intravenously for acetaminophen overdose, which is genuinely life-saving medicine. But at oral doses of 600 to 1200 mg per day, the evidence base for supplemental use is actually quite decent. A 2015 meta-analysis published in Respiratory Medicine found that NAC supplementation significantly reduced exacerbation rates in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease compared to placebo, with a relative risk reduction of around 0.75 across the pooled studies. That's not a marginal effect. For more on this, see The FDA's 2023 Crackdown on NAD+ Supplements Was a Wake-Up Call the Industry Needed.

There's also a body of work on NAC and exercise recovery. In my work with competitive cyclists and triathletes, I've seen athletes respond well to NAC in the 48 hours following high-volume training, particularly when markers like creatine kinase are elevated. I wouldn't say it's transformative for everyone, but the mechanistic rationale is sound. The glutathione replenishment pathway is real, the oral bioavailability is measurable, and the safety profile at standard doses is genuinely good. The NIH's LiverTox database rates NAC as having a very low risk of hepatotoxicity, which is more than you can say for plenty of herbal extracts that were never questioned under DSHEA.

None of that science entered Amazon's calculation. The platform made a business decision, not a scientific one, and the consumers who paid for it were people using NAC for entirely reasonable purposes.

The Industry Pushed Back, and Not Always Coherently

The Natural Products Association filed formal comments urging the FDA to clarify NAC's status as a lawful supplement. The Council for Responsible Nutrition did the same. Several brands kept selling through their own direct-to-consumer channels while Amazon listings stayed dark. That split approach inadvertently illustrated how much supplement retail had become dependent on a single platform, and how little regulatory certainty exists for ingredients that straddle the drug-supplement boundary.

I'll be honest here... I found the industry's public messaging in mid-2021 a bit all over the place. Some companies leaned hard into the "FDA overreach" narrative, which has some merit but also obscures the real issue: DSHEA's drug-preclusion clause exists for a reason, and the supplement industry has historically benefited from flexible FDA enforcement. Crying foul when that flexibility runs the wrong direction feels a bit convenient. My friend Marcus, who runs a small sports nutrition brand out of Leeds and exports to the US market, texted me in May 2021 genuinely baffled about whether to pull his NAC product proactively or wait for clarity. He waited. Three of his competitors pulled and then relisted within six weeks. Nobody actually knew what the right move was, and I'm not sure the lawyers they were paying did either.

The FTC's activity that same year added another layer. The crackdown on supplement advertising had been building momentum. For a full picture of how the regulatory environment stacked up in 2021, the FTC's 2021 enforcement actions on supplement claims are worth reading alongside this NAC situation, because together they show an agency environment that was simultaneously aggressive and inconsistent. For more on this, see The FDA's 2019 Warning Letter Surge: What It Really Meant for the Supplement Industry.

What the NAC Episode Actually Revealed About Supplement Regulation

The core problem isn't NAC. It's that DSHEA's framework was written in 1994 and it creaks under the weight of modern pharmacology. Ingredients now move fluidly between drug pipelines and supplement markets, sometimes simultaneously, and the law doesn't have good mechanisms for that. The FDA acknowledged in its 2021 statement that it was considering whether to use its discretionary authority to allow NAC to remain on the market anyway, which is a tacit admission that the strict legal reading produces an outcome nobody actually wants.

By late 2021, the FDA had still not issued a final rule. NAC remained in a limbo that benefited nobody: not consumers, not brands, not the agency itself. Amazon eventually began reinstating some NAC listings as the legal picture failed to resolve into a clear prohibition, but the damage to smaller brands' sales data for Q2 2021 was real and unrecoverable. Some estimates from industry analysts put the direct sales disruption in the tens of millions of dollars across the affected product category, though I haven't seen a precise independent audit of that figure.

The FTC's earlier work on tightening supplement advertising standards, detailed in this piece on the FTC's 2020 supplement advertising crackdown, at least produced clearer guidance that brands could act on. The NAC situation produced the opposite: a documented uncertainty that the FDA explicitly declined to resolve quickly. That's the part that should concern you as a consumer or a practitioner. An agency that moves fast on marketing claims but slowly on ingredient legality is an agency optimised for the wrong problem.

  • NAC's safety profile at oral doses of 600 to 1200 mg per day is well-documented and not meaningfully contested.
  • The drug-preclusion argument hinges on a historical marketing question the FDA has not conclusively answered.
  • Amazon's preemptive removal was a platform decision, not a regulatory mandate, and those two things got conflated badly in media coverage.

My position is simple: the FDA should formalise NAC's supplement status rather than leave the market in permanent grey-zone uncertainty. The science supports it. The safety record supports it. And the alternative, a regulatory ambiguity that large brands can absorb but small ones can't, is exactly the kind of structural unfairness that erodes trust in supplement oversight across the board.

If you're a practitioner recommending NAC, or a consumer using it, check that your supplier is operating through direct-to-consumer channels with clear labelling and ideally third-party testing through NSF or Informed Sport. The regulatory status hasn't been resolved as of the end of 2021, and that means your due diligence on sourcing matters more than usual. Frequently asked questions

Is NAC still legal to sell as a supplement in the US?

As of the end of 2021, the FDA had not issued a final rule excluding NAC from supplement status. The situation remained unresolved, with the agency indicating it might use discretionary enforcement. Some retailers reinstated listings, but the legal question was not formally closed.

Why did Amazon remove NAC supplements?

Amazon acted preemptively after the FDA acknowledged citizen petitions arguing NAC is excluded from supplement status under DSHEA because it was approved as a drug before being marketed as a supplement. Amazon's removal was a platform policy decision, not a direct FDA enforcement action.

Does NAC actually work as a supplement, or is it just hype?

The evidence for NAC at standard oral doses is reasonably solid, particularly for respiratory health and glutathione replenishment. A 2015 meta-analysis found meaningful reductions in COPD exacerbation rates. It's not a magic bullet, but the mechanistic basis and safety record are both credible at 600 to 1200 mg per day.
