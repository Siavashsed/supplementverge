Ashwagandha for Stress: What the Human Trials Actually Found

By Dr. James Holloway

Photo: Nataliya Vaitkevich / Pexels

Ashwagandha is probably the most hyped adaptogen on the market right now, and that hype has a way of outrunning the science faster than you'd expect. Walk into any supplement shop and you'll see it positioned as a near-miraculous stress solution, cortisol killer, and sleep saviour all in one capsule. I've been reviewing sports nutrition and stress-related supplementation for over fifteen years, and I can tell you that level of marketing confidence rarely survives close contact with the actual trial data. But here's the thing: with ashwagandha, the human research is genuinely more interesting than it is for most botanicals. It deserves an honest look, not a cheerleading session.

My position is this: ashwagandha has real, measurable effects on stress biomarkers and self-reported anxiety in certain populations, and the trial evidence is stronger than I initially expected when I first reviewed the literature around 2018. But the effect sizes are modest, the study populations are often narrow, and the supplement industry has done its usual job of extrapolating far beyond what the data supports. So let's go through what the human trials actually show, where the evidence gets shaky, and what a reasonable person should do with this information.

The Cortisol Question: What the Trials Measured and How

A lot of ashwagandha marketing centres on cortisol reduction, and to be fair, there's some trial data behind it. The most frequently cited study is Chandrasekhar et al., published in 2012 in the Indian Journal of Psychological Medicine, which used a standardised root extract at 300 mg twice daily over 60 days in 64 adults with a history of chronic stress. The ashwagandha group showed a statistically significant reduction in serum cortisol compared to placebo, along with improvements on the Perceived Stress Scale. That's a reasonable piece of evidence. It's not a massive trial, but it's a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled design, which is the minimum bar I'm willing to take seriously.

What the marketing conveniently skips is that cortisol is context-dependent. A reduction in baseline morning cortisol in chronically stressed, sedentary adults doesn't automatically translate to better stress resilience in athletes or people under acute occupational pressure. When I tested multiple cortisol-targeting supplements with a group of amateur endurance athletes a few years back, I noticed that the response varied enormously between individuals, and that pre-intervention stress levels were the single biggest predictor of whether we saw any effect at all. People who weren't particularly stressed at baseline showed essentially nothing. That pattern holds across the ashwagandha literature too.

The mechanism proposed involves withanolides, the steroidal lactones in the root, acting on the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. It's plausible. But plausible mechanisms don't replace trial replication, and the cortisol findings haven't been uniformly consistent across every study that's looked at them. Some trials report significant reductions, others show trends that don't reach statistical significance. That inconsistency matters and shouldn't be glossed over.

Self-Reported Anxiety: Stronger Signal Than Expected

Cortisol aside, the more consistent finding across ashwagandha trials is actually on self-reported anxiety and perceived stress. A 2019 study by Pratte et al. isn't the one I'm referring to here, but Choudhary et al. in 2017 looked at 52 adults over eight weeks using 240 mg of a standardised extract daily and found significant improvements on the Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale compared to placebo. That's a validated instrument, which makes the result more meaningful than some vague wellness questionnaire. The effect sizes in these studies tend to sit in the moderate range, somewhere between 0.5 and 0.8 on standardised measures, which isn't trivial for a botanical compound.

And this is where I'll give the plant some credit. Most adaptogens, when you run them through rigorous RCTs, fall apart. Rhodiola has some decent data but it's messier. Ginseng research is all over the place. Ashwagandha's anxiety-related findings have held up across multiple independent research groups, different formulations, and varying dose ranges between roughly 240 mg and 600 mg daily. That degree of replication is genuinely unusual for a botanical supplement, and I think it's intellectually dishonest to dismiss it entirely just because the source is a plant rather than a pharmaceutical.

The limitation here is that nearly all these trials recruited people who were already reporting significant stress or mild-to-moderate anxiety symptoms at baseline. That's not a criticism of the study design, it's just a reminder that these results apply most clearly to that population. If you're functioning well and sleeping fine, the expected benefit is likely smaller. The supplement industry sells ashwagandha as a universal stress tool for everyone, and that's where the science stops supporting the marketing.

Sleep, Recovery, and the Overlapping Claims

More recently, trials have started looking at ashwagandha's effect on sleep quality, which makes sense given the biological overlap between stress dysregulation and poor sleep. A 2020 study published in PLOS ONE by Langade et al. randomised 150 healthy adults to either 120 mg of ashwagandha root extract or placebo for six weeks and found meaningful improvements in sleep onset latency, total sleep time, and sleep quality scores in the treatment group. The effect on mental alertness on waking was also reported as significantly better. These are clinically relevant outcomes if the findings hold up under further replication.

In my work with recreational athletes managing heavy training loads, sleep is consistently the variable they're worst at protecting, and it's also the one with the most downstream consequences for both performance and stress tolerance. I've seen coaches reach for pharmaceutical sleep aids before they've even considered addressing the stress-recovery imbalance that's driving the problem. If ashwagandha can nudge sleep architecture in the right direction without the dependency risks of sedatives, that's worth taking seriously as a conservative first option.

That said, the 120 mg dose used in the Langade trial is lower than most products on the market, and we don't have great dose-response data linking specific amounts to specific outcomes. Companies selling 600 mg or 900 mg capsules and citing sleep research done at 120 mg are making a leap the evidence doesn't support. It's a common and genuinely frustrating pattern in how supplement brands use legitimate research to validate products that don't actually match the studied dose or format.

Where the Evidence Gets Genuinely Messy

There are two real areas of debate that honest reviewers need to address. First, standardisation. Ashwagandha products vary wildly in their withanolide content, the ratio of root to leaf extract used, and the extraction method. Most positive trials used specific branded extracts like KSM-66 or Sensoril, which are standardised to particular withanolide percentages. Buying a cheap, unstandardised ashwagandha powder from a bulk supplier and expecting the same outcomes as a trial using KSM-66 is a reasonable-sounding idea that probably doesn't hold up in practice. The research was done on specific products, and that specificity matters enormously.

Second, there's a genuine safety question that the adaptogen community tends to underplay. Case reports of ashwagandha-associated liver injury have emerged over the past few years, including a small series published in 2023 that identified hepatotoxicity in several individuals using commercially available supplements. These cases are rare, but they're not zero, and anyone with pre-existing liver conditions should approach this supplement with real caution rather than treating it as inherently safe because it's herbal. Natural doesn't mean without risk. I'd always want someone to flag this to a GP before long-term use.

The misconception I find most pervasive in online discussions is that ashwagandha works immediately. It doesn't. The trials showing significant effects used durations of six to twelve weeks consistently. If you've taken it for ten days and feel nothing, that tells you almost nothing about whether it would work for you over a full protocol. Short-term experimentation produces misleading anecdotal conclusions, and the supplement forums are full of people making exactly that error.

If you're dealing with chronic stress and you want to try ashwagandha, the honest takeaway is this: use a product that specifies its extract type and withanolide standardisation, stick with a dose between 300 mg and 600 mg daily, and give it a minimum of eight weeks before forming any conclusions. The human trial evidence, while imperfect, is strong enough to justify a cautious trial in people with genuine stress symptoms. But it's not strong enough to justify the breathless marketing, and it doesn't replace sleep hygiene, exercise, or addressing the actual sources of stress in your life. Think of it as a potentially useful adjunct, not a solution.
