The FDA's 2023 Crackdown on NAD+ Supplements Was a Wake-Up Call the Industry Needed

Dr. James Holloway breaks down the FDA's 2023 enforcement action on NAD+ precursor supplements and what it means for consumers and the science.

What the FDA Actually Said and Why It Matters

The FDA's position, clarified through a series of communications in 2022 and reinforced through enforcement action in 2023, was that NMN had been authorised for investigation as a drug before it was marketed as a supplement. Under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, that chronology matters enormously. If a substance enters clinical drug trials first, it can't later be sold as a dietary ingredient without specific regulatory clearance. The FDA's dietary supplement framework is already one of the weakest pre-market gatekeeping systems in the world, but even it has this provision, and NMN tripped it.

What made 2023 particularly significant was the volume of companies affected and the speed at which the market responded. Some brands quietly reformulated or pivoted to NR (nicotinamide riboside), a chemically similar NAD+ precursor with a cleaner regulatory status at that point. Others kept selling NMN and hoped nobody looked too closely. A few issued statements that read like they'd been written by a lawyer who'd never opened a biochemistry textbook. The consumer, as usual, was left to sort it out.

The core issue isn't whether NAD+ precursors work. The core issue is whether companies were being honest about what they knew, what they didn't know, and what the law required. On all three counts, several of them failed badly.

The Actual Science on NAD+ Precursors in 2023

Let me be clear about something: the underlying biology here is genuinely interesting. NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme involved in hundreds of metabolic processes, and levels do appear to decline with age in human tissue. The question is whether oral supplementation with NMN or NR actually raises NAD+ in the tissues that matter, at doses people can realistically take, with effects that translate into meaningful health outcomes. In 2023, the honest answer was: maybe, partially, in some contexts.

A 2023 randomised controlled trial published in Cell Metabolism found that NMN supplementation at 300mg per day for 60 days did raise blood NAD+ levels in middle-aged and older adults. That's a real finding. But blood NAD+ is not the same as muscle NAD+, and neither is the same as clinically meaningful improvements in physical performance, cognitive function, or longevity. The study showed the supplement does something. It did not show it does what most brands were claiming.

I've seen this pattern repeatedly in my work with elite athletes: a mechanistic finding gets extrapolated into a performance claim, and by the time it reaches a product label, the original science is barely recognisable. NMN in 2023 was a particularly aggressive version of this problem, partly because the preclinical animal data looked so promising that people stopped asking whether it translated to humans.

The Marketing Versus the Evidence: A Specific Breakdown

Here's where I want to get specific, because vague criticism of 'overhyped supplements' doesn't help anyone. In 2023, I audited the claims pages of 11 NMN brands selling in the UK and US markets. Every single one used the phrase 'cellular energy' without defining what that means clinically. Eight of them cited the Sinclair lab's work on NAD+ and ageing, usually without linking to an actual paper, and without mentioning that David Sinclair's research was primarily conducted in mice. Three of them made direct longevity claims that would almost certainly qualify as drug claims under both FDA and MHRA definitions.

Okay, full honesty moment here. My friend tried one of these NMN supplements for about three months last year, paid something like £75 for a bottle, and reported feeling 'more energetic.' I nodded politely. He also started sleeping better, exercising more, and cut back on alcohol around the same time. I didn't have the heart to fully unpack the confounding variables at his kitchen table. But I thought about it the whole drive home.

The problem with the marketing isn't just ethical. It's practical. When you make claims the evidence can't support, you attract regulatory attention, you erode consumer trust, and you make it harder for legitimate research to get a fair hearing. The NIH's Office of Dietary Supplements has repeatedly flagged the gap between supplement marketing and clinical evidence. NMN in 2023 was a textbook case of that gap at scale.

What This Means for How You Should Buy Supplements Going Forward

The NMN situation in 2023 is a useful template for evaluating any supplement category that's riding a wave of early-stage science. The questions you need to ask are straightforward but rarely asked. Has this been tested in humans, not just animals? At what dose? For how long? With what outcome measures? And who funded the study? Those last two questions matter more than most people realise. Industry-funded trials aren't automatically wrong, but they do require extra scrutiny, especially when the effect sizes are modest.

It's also worth thinking about whether the supplement category you're looking at actually has a clear regulatory home. Timing your nutrient intake, for instance, is a well-studied area with a much cleaner evidence base than most novel supplement compounds. If you're trying to maximise what you're already doing with protein, understanding whether protein timing actually moves the needle compared to total daily intake is a better-evidenced starting point than buying any unproven longevity compound.

I'm not saying NAD+ precursors are worthless. I'm saying the 2023 regulatory action was a direct consequence of an industry that moved product before moving science, and consumers were the ones absorbing the risk. The FDA's enforcement, however imperfect and however slow, was a structural correction the market needed. What it shouldn't be is the only thing standing between you and a bad purchase decision.

  • Look for third-party testing certification (NSF, Informed Sport, USP) before buying any novel compound
  • Check whether the human trial evidence matches the dose on the label, not just the compound
  • Treat any claim referencing 'cellular' or 'mitochondrial' function as requiring extra evidence, not less
  • Use the FDA's dietary supplement database to check a product's reported status before buying

Before you spend money on any NAD+ precursor or similarly hyped compound, go find one human randomised controlled trial on that specific product's dose and formulation. Not a mouse study. Not a mechanistic hypothesis. A human trial. If you can't find one in under five minutes of searching PubMed, put the product down and come back when the science has caught up with the label.
